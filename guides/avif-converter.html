Why Convert to AVIF?

AVIF (AV1 Image File Format) represents the cutting edge of image compression technology. Based on the AV1 video codec, it delivers exceptional compression efficiency that surpasses both JPEG and WebP.

  • Superior Compression: AVIF achieves 30-50% smaller file sizes than JPEG at the same quality level. Compared to WebP, AVIF typically saves an additional 20-30% without sacrificing visual quality.
  • HDR and Wide Color Gamut: AVIF supports High Dynamic Range and wide color gamuts like BT.2020 and P3. This allows it to represent colors and luminance levels that JPEG and WebP cannot, making it future-proof for modern displays.
  • High Color Depth: AVIF supports 8-bit, 10-bit, and 12-bit color depths. This eliminates banding in smooth gradients and provides better color accuracy for professional photography and design work.
  • Transparency Support: AVIF supports alpha channel transparency with the same advanced compression as its color data, making transparent images more efficient than PNG or even WebP in many cases.
  • Open Standard, Royalty-Free: AVIF is an open, royalty-free format developed by the Alliance for Open Media. It has no licensing costs or patent restrictions, making it attractive for web standards adoption.

AVIF vs WebP vs JPEG: The Comparison

Understanding how AVIF stacks up against existing formats helps you decide when to adopt it and what tradeoffs to expect.

  • Compression Efficiency: AVIF consistently produces the smallest files of any widely supported format. Test images show AVIF at quality 50 looks comparable to JPEG at quality 80, at half the file size.
  • Encoding Speed: AVIF encoding is significantly slower than either JPEG or WebP. A single high-resolution image can take 3-10 seconds to encode in the browser. This is a limitation of the computationally intensive AV1 codec.
  • Browser Support: As of 2026, AVIF enjoys broad support across Chrome, Firefox, Safari, and Edge covering approximately 92% of users. This is good but not universal, requiring fallback strategies.
  • Feature Set: AVIF supports features no other web image format offers: HDR, 12-bit color, lossless and lossy modes, monochrome, and multi-frame animation (AVIF sequence).

Tip: AVIF is best suited for high-value images where quality and file size matter most: hero images, product photos, and large banners. For thumbnails and decorative images, WebP or JPEG may be more practical due to faster encoding.

Step-by-Step: Convert to AVIF

Step 1: Upload Your Source Image

What to do: Click the upload area or drag and drop your JPEG, PNG, or WebP file. The tool loads it into your browser and displays a preview with original file details.

Why it matters: The preview shows your starting quality. AVIF works best from high-quality sources since it can preserve more detail at lower file sizes than other formats.

Common mistake: Uploading a low-quality or heavily compressed JPEG. The AVIF output will still contain the original artifacts. Start from the highest quality source available.

Step 2: Set Quality Level

What to do: Adjust the quality slider. AVIF quality ranges work differently than JPEG. A quality setting of 50-60 in AVIF often matches or exceeds JPEG quality 80-90 in visual appearance.

Why it matters: AVIFs efficiency means you can use lower quality settings than you are used to with JPEG. Experiment with values between 40 and 70 for the best balance of size and quality.

Common mistake: Using JPEG-quality expectations with AVIF. Drop your quality setting by 20-30 points compared to your JPEG habit. Quality 50 in AVIF is often visually lossless for photographs.

Step 3: Wait for Encoding

What to do: Click the convert button and wait for the encoding to complete. AVIF encoding takes longer than other formats. A progress indicator shows the encoding status.

Why it matters: The encoding time reflects the advanced compression algorithms working to create the smallest possible file. The wait is typically 2-10 seconds for standard images.

Common mistake: Navigating away or closing the tab during encoding. If the encoding is interrupted, you will need to start over. Be patient the result is worth the wait.

Step 4: Preview the Result

What to do: Review the converted AVIF image alongside the original. Compare file sizes and inspect for any visible quality differences.

Why it matters: The side-by-side comparison confirms that the image quality is acceptable. AVIFs efficiency can make surprisingly low quality settings look great.

Common mistake: Only comparing file sizes. Check zoomed-in areas of the image for artifacts, especially in smooth gradients, shadows, and high-frequency detail areas.

Step 5: Download and Implement

What to do: Download your AVIF file. When implementing on a website, use the picture element with fallback formats for browsers that do not support AVIF.

Why it matters: Proper implementation ensures all users see your images. The picture element approach is the standard for serving next-gen formats with graceful degradation.

Common mistake: Replacing all existing images with AVIF without a fallback plan. Always serve a fallback format (JPEG or WebP) beneath your AVIF source.

Best Practices for AVIF Conversion

Tip: Always provide fallbacks using the picture element. Use <source> with type="image/avif" followed by a WebP or JPEG fallback. Browsers will choose the first format they support, ensuring everyone gets a compatible image.

Tip: Use AVIF for hero images and large photos. The compression savings are most dramatic on large, high-resolution images. A 2 MB hero JPEG might become 600 KB as AVIF with indistinguishable quality.

Tip: Test encoding times before batch processing. If you plan to convert hundreds of images, test a few first to understand the time investment. AVIF encoding can be 10-50x slower than JPEG encoding.

Tip: Dont use AVIF for transient or trivial images. For thumbnails, icons, or disposable images, WebP or JPEG is more practical. Reserve AVIF for images where quality and file size truly matter.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: No Fallback for Unsupported Browsers

The Problem: Using AVIF exclusively without fallbacks, leaving approximately 8% of users unable to see your images.

Why it Fails: Despite broad support, some users on older browsers or specific configurations cannot decode AVIF. These users will see broken image placeholders.

Fix: Always use the picture element with a WebP or JPEG fallback type. This is a simple HTML change that ensures universal compatibility.

Mistake 2: Converting Lossy to AVIF Multiple Times

The Problem: Converting JPEG to AVIF, then back to JPEG, then to AVIF again, creating generational quality loss.

Why it Fails: AVIF is lossy like JPEG. Converting to AVIF discards information. Converting back and forth compounds the loss, resulting in rapidly degrading image quality.

Fix: Convert once from your highest-quality source and keep that source archived. Convert to AVIF as a final delivery step, not as an intermediate format.

Mistake 3: Not Accounting for Encoding Time

The Problem: Expecting instantaneous conversion and becoming frustrated when AVIF encoding takes 5-10 seconds per image.

Why it Fails: AV1 encoding is computationally intensive even for still images. Browser-based encoding using WebAssembly (as in our tool) adds additional overhead compared to native encoding.

Fix: Plan for longer conversion times. Process images one at a time or batch them and let the tool work through each one sequentially. The file size savings justify the wait.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which browsers support AVIF?

AVIF is supported by Chrome 85+, Firefox 93+, Safari 16.1+, and Opera 71+. As of 2026, approximately 92% of browsers support AVIF. Internet Explorer and some older mobile browsers do not. Always provide JPEG or WebP fallbacks for maximum compatibility.

How does AVIF compare to WebP?

AVIF typically achieves 20-30% better compression than WebP at equivalent visual quality. It also supports higher color depths (10/12-bit), HDR, and wider color gamuts. However, AVIF encoding is significantly slower than WebP, and browser support is slightly less comprehensive.

Can AVIF handle transparency?

Yes, AVIF supports alpha channel transparency just like PNG and WebP. The transparent areas are compressed efficiently, typically resulting in smaller files than PNG with transparency and comparable to or better than WebP with transparency.

Is AVIF encoding slow?

AVIF encoding is substantially slower than JPEG or WebP encoding because it uses the more computationally complex AV1 codec. A single image may take several seconds to encode in the browser. This is expected and the tradeoff is better compression. Batch processing may take longer.

Is my image uploaded to a server during conversion?

No. The entire conversion process happens locally in your browser using JavaScript. Your image never leaves your device and is not uploaded to any server.
